The moment I realized I had a bookmarking problem was not dramatic. There was no crash, no catastrophic data loss. I was simply trying to write an article and couldn’t remember where the key quote was.

I knew I had saved it somewhere. I remembered the article, roughly. It had something to do with energy policy, or maybe climate economics. I had bookmarked it weeks earlier because one paragraph explained the issue perfectly. But when I opened my browser bookmarks, I found what most journalists eventually accumulate: a long, unsorted archive of good intentions. Hundreds of links. Many with vague titles. Most saved “for later.”

Later had arrived, and the useful part was buried.

Like many reporters and writers, I had built a patchwork system to deal with research. I saved links in bookmarks. I copied quotes into a notes app. I tracked story ideas in another document. RSS feeds lived in yet another tool. When it came time to write, I bounced between tabs, documents, and half-remembered highlights trying to reconstruct what I had already read.

The real problem wasn’t finding articles. The internet is very good at giving us more articles than we can possibly read. The problem was turning those articles into something usable when the writing started.

Saving a link is easy. Remembering why it mattered is harder.

The more I thought about it, the clearer the pattern became. My workflow had several distinct steps: finding articles, reading them, highlighting important passages, organizing them around a topic, and eventually turning those fragments into a story. But none of my tools treated those steps as part of the same process. Bookmarks stored links. Notes stored text. Drafts lived somewhere else entirely.

Every step required copying information from one place to another.

So I started building something for myself, initially just to remove that friction. I wanted a place where an article didn’t stop being useful the moment I saved it. Instead of storing the link, the tool would pull in the article itself and display it in a clean reader without the ads, pop-ups, and shifting layouts that clutter most websites.

Reading it inside the app felt closer to working with a document than visiting a webpage. And once it looked like a document, the next step felt obvious: highlighting.

Being able to mark sentences or paragraphs changed the way I interacted with sources. Instead of bookmarking an article because it might contain something useful, I could capture the exact passages that mattered. Over time those highlights started to feel like the real research material, while the article itself became context.

But highlights alone didn’t solve the problem. Reporting rarely revolves around a single article. Most stories pull from dozens of sources scattered across different publications. What I really needed was a way to connect those sources to the stories I was trying to write.

That’s where projects came in. Instead of treating bookmarks as a flat list, I started grouping articles around specific ideas or reporting threads. An investigation, a feature concept, a topic I wanted to follow. Articles could belong to more than one project, which meant a source about climate policy could also live inside a broader energy story.

Once that structure existed, something interesting happened. Instead of reopening articles one by one to find my highlights, the project view could show the important passages gathered from all of them. The scattered fragments of research started to look like raw material for a narrative.

Writing suddenly felt closer.

Another piece of the puzzle was discovery. Like many journalists, I rely on RSS feeds to keep track of publications and newsletters. I didn’t want a separate reader for that. I wanted to see new articles, open them in the same clean reading environment, and save the ones worth deeper work.

Gradually the pieces came together into a single place where the whole research loop could happen. Find articles. Read them without distractions. Highlight the parts that matter. Organize them around the story you’re building. And when the moment comes to start writing, open the project and see your source material waiting there.

One design decision became important early on. I didn’t want the tool to require accounts or store research in a remote cloud service. A lot of journalistic work involves unfinished reporting and sensitive notes. Keeping the data stored locally in the browser felt simpler and more respectful of that reality. The tool behaves less like a social platform and more like a personal workspace.

Eventually I added a small writing editor inside each project. It’s not meant to replace full-featured writing software, but it creates a bridge between research and the first draft. While writing, I can see the highlights from all my sources in a sidebar. A quote can move from article to draft in seconds, and if I want to check the context I can open the source immediately.

The whole process feels closer to spreading annotated documents across a desk than digging through a pile of bookmarks.

What started as a personal fix for my own messy workflow gradually turned into a real application: Bookmark Manager. It’s still a simple idea at heart. Instead of treating bookmarks as an archive of links, it treats them as the starting point of a writing process.

The link is only the beginning. The real value comes from what you extract from it.

I’m now offering the app free to journalists who want to try it, partly because reporters are the people who most clearly understand this problem. If the tool is going to work, it needs to fit the way research actually happens: messy, nonlinear, and constantly evolving.

Most of us don’t suffer from a lack of information. We suffer from too much of it, scattered across too many places.

For years my browser bookmarks were a quiet reminder of that. Now, at least, they’ve turned into something closer to a working library. And more importantly, they finally help me get from a saved article to a finished story.
